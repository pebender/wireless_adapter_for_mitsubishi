# My Background

## My Education

After my parents divorced, my mom, my two sisters and I moved to [Santa Clara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Santa_Clara,_California) and my dad moved to [La Jolla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/La_Jolla). It was the summer of my 7th birthday.

So, I had the luck of growing up in [Silicon Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley) during the 1970s and early 1980s. In Silicon Valley, there were stores that sold used electronic parts and equipment. In addition, Radio Shack still existed and still sold electronic parts.

I had the further luck of spending three years in [John McCollum's electronics class](https://www.eetimes.com/john-mccollum-hs-teacher-taught-electronics-with-love/) at [Homestead High School](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homestead_High_School_(California)) (but fifteen years after Steve Wozniak and eleven years after Steve Jobs ... I'm not that old). Thanks to John McCollum, my interest in and understanding of electronics (especially digital electronics) grew.

After high school, I studied electrical engineering at [University of California San Diego (UCSD)](https://ucsd.edu). I completed a BS as well as a PhD. As an undergraduate student, I studied [communication theory and systems](https://www.ece.ucsd.edu/faculty-research/ece-research-areas/communication-theory-systems). As a graduate student, I studied [information theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_theory) under [Jack Wolf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jack_Wolf).

After college, I worked at [Qualcomm](https://www.qualcomm.com) as a systems engineer from 1992-2014. During my time there, I worked with and learned from many bright people, including [Andy Viterbi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andrew_Viterbi), [Roberto Padovani](https://jacobsschool.ucsd.edu/people/profile/roberto-padovani),
Franklin Antonio, and of course [Irwin Jacobs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irwin_M._Jacobs).

## My Interests

Generally, I like to figure out how things work and to learn new things. However, I'm most interested in electronics and home automation as well as in the software needed to make it work.

Apparently, I've always been interested in how things work. My mom says I would take things apart so that I could figure out how they worked. However, I couldn't put the thing back together some (most) of the time.

Since I was in elementary school, I have been interested in electronics. Of course I built a crystal radio set. Of course I tried to increase the range of my
[Walkie-Talkies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walkie-talkie) by connecting them to an antenna on the roof. Of course I tried to automate light switches and electrical outlets, resulting in a few blown glass fuses.

And I loved building non-electronic things as well. My elementary school friend and I built multiple wooden forts at my house: one in the rafters of my garage, and more than one in the trees in my backyard. And I built things with [Lego](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lego). And I built things with [Erector Set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erector_Set).

I loved taking shop classes.

In seventh grade at [Cabrillo Middle School](https://cabrillo.santaclarausd.org), I took metal shop and wood shop. I found out that I am afraid of power tools in general and circular saws in particular. To this day, I often do projects with the less than optimal tool because power tools make me nervous. I occasionally do projects involving wood, and the power drill and the jig saw still make me nervous. And I don't own a circular saw.

With the exception of shop classes, I was bored in middle school. I had already completed all the math material the middle school had to offer while I was in elementary school, and I found all the other subjects except shop boring. So I was often truant and sometimes in trouble. Through the efforts of my mom, I skipped eighth grade and started ninth grade at [Peterson High School](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peterson_High_School) where my mom taught.

I still have my old-school drafting equipment, but rarely use it. In the summer after 9th grade, we moved from Santa Clara to Los Altos resulting in me changing from Peterson High School to [Homestead High School](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homestead_High_School_(California)). In addition to having access to more advanced math classes, I had access to more shop classes. I took auto shop and electronics, and I used the metal shop's equipment to help with building my electronics projects. Sadly, [my electronics teacher passed away in 1990](https://www.findagrave.com/memorial/58653313/john-claude-mccollum), and [the shop building was torn down in 2019](https://www.mercurynews.com/2019/10/05/homesteads-hub-is-a-place-for-collaboration/).

## My Digital Projects

### My First Digital Circuit (While a Homestead High School Student)

The first digital hardware I designed and built was a computer in 1982-1983 during my senior year of high school.

After spending my sophomore year learning about analog hardware and my junior year learning about digital hardware, my electronics class lab partner and I spent my senior year designing and building a computer. The computer's design was constrained by the parts our electronics teacher had acquired from various local companies Because the parts bin contained a [Z80A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zilog_Z80) and numerous [7400 series ICs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/7400-series_integrated_circuits), our initial design was similar to the [TRS-80](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TRS-80). However, after finding a video display controller (VDC) in the parts bin (I don't remember the part but it could have been the [TMS9918A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TMS9918) based on its capabilities), we changed the design. The final design used the Z80A and the VDC, each with 16-kilobytes of RAM. The PCB was a hand-built, two layer board. We

- hand drew the schematic on paper,
- hand drew the layout on paper,
- transferred the layout to transparencies using a photocopier,
- transferred the layout to a copper clad board using photo-resist and sunlight,
- etched off the exposed copper using some sort of chemical bath,
- drilled the dual inline package (DIP) through holes using the metal shop's drill press, and
- hand soldered the components to the board.

### My Next Digital Project (While a UCSD Undergraduate Student)

The next digital hardware I designed was during the summer of 1987 as an intern at [NCR](https://www.ncr.com). It relied primarily on 7400 series ICs. However, I access to schematic capture, logic simulation and PCB layout software (anyone else remember [Daisy Systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daisy_Systems)). And after completing the PCB layout, I was able to have it auto-magically fabricated and assembled.

### My Last Digital Project (While a UCSD Graduate Student)

The last digital hardware I designed was during the 1989-1990 and 1990-1991 academic years as part of my PhD research. It was a board for quantifying the potential gains of using Reed-Solomon error correction codes on magnetic hard disk drives. Initially, the design used Advanced Hardware Architectures' AHA4010 Reed-Solomon ASICs (which were donated to us) along with numerous 7400 series ICs. The board was wire-wrapped by my sardonic, hard working, lab assistant. Unfortunately, it never ran at speed (30MHz) reliably. Thankfully, my advisor was willing to spend the money for the relatively costly Xilinx hardware and associated design software (anyone else remember FutureNet). The final design used two AHA4010 ASICs along with three Xilinx XC3042 FPGAs, was still wire-wrapped by my lab assistant, and ran at speed.

## My Other Projects

As a systems engineer at Qualcomm, I was more removed from the hardware and the software. I worked on the architectures and algorithms that the hardware and software would implement, but other people did the hardware and software designs and implementations.

And my personal projects became software centric:

- I wrote software to control my Lansonic DAS-750 networked MP3 player with my Compaq iPAQ PDA,
- I wrote software to control my Sony Plasma TV through its RS-232 serial port,
- I wrote software to assist my home church with projecting worship song lyrics,
- I maintained a Linux based distribution that turned diskless a VIA EPIA M based computer into MythTV frontend,
- I wrote software to make TV remote controls more plug and play under Linux,
- I wrote software to control my LG LCD TV through its websocket interface, and
- I contributed fixes to various Open Source software on which my home network and my software projects depended.

## My Life Phases

After completing my PhD, my life went through multiple phases:

1. I got married, I started working at Qualcomm and we bought the first home,
2. We had two boys, I took non-engineering assignments at Qualcomm, we sold the first home and we bought the second home,
3. I sufferend autistic burnout, I got mis-diagnosed with bipolar type 2, I got divorced and I was let go from Qualcomm,
4. I got married, we bought the third home and I continued to suffer from autistic burnout, and
5. the boys left home for college, I got officially diagnosed with autism and I recovered somewhat from the autistic burnout.

During the first phase, I had a lot of time to spend on personal projects, including home networking, home automation and Open Source software projects. During the second phase, I had less time to spend on personal projects. During the third phase, I lacked motivation to spend time personal projects. During the fourth phase, I still lacked motivation. During the fifth phase, I have had more time and more motivation to work on personal projects, including home networking, home automation and Open Source projects. During this time, I learned [Rust](https://rust-lang.org), I learned embedded develop on the ESP32 family using C on [esp-idf](https://docs.espressif.com/projects/esp-idf/en/stable/esp32/index.html) and Rust on [esp-hal](https://github.com/esp-rs/esp-hal)

During the first phase, the solutions were more DIY, partially because I had more time and partially because there were few/no turnkey options available. During the second, third and fourth phases, the solutions shifted toward more turnkey, partially because I had less time and partially because I had less motivation. During the fifth phase, the solutions have been a combination of turnkey and DIY.
