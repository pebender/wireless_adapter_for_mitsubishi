# Our Multimedia Home

## Migrating Away from Music CDs

In the mid 1980s, I migrated from vinyl to CDs.

In the late 1990s, I converted my CD collection to MP3s using [Exact Audio Copy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exact_Audio_Copy). I burned MP3s to CD ROMs using
[Nero Burning ROM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nero_Burning_ROM) so that I could listen to them at work and later my car. My car stereo had a CD player could read a CD ROM and could play MP3 files.

I purchased various portable MP3 players so that I could listen to them while running, including

- a [Creative Nomad MuVo2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Creative_Nomad),
- an [iRiver Prism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IRiver) (don't remember the model),
- a [SanDisk Sansa Clip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SanDisk_portable_media_players#Sansa_Clip), and
- a [SanDisk Sansa Clip+](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SanDisk_portable_media_players#Sansa_Clip+).

I stayed with the SanDisk Sansa Clip+ until wireless charging came to the Apple iPhone and the Apple AirPods. The convenience of no headphone wires, the ease of wireless changing and the fact that my iPhone is always with me caused me to retire my SanDisk Sansa Clip+ and use my iPhone as my only portable MP3 player.

Once I had wired Ethernet throughout the house, I bought

- a [Lansonic DAS-750](https://web.archive.org/web/20001002044306/http://www.lansonic.com/product.htm),
- a [Slim Devices SLiMP3](https://wiki.lyrion.org/index.php/SLIMP3.html), and
- a [Logitech SqueezeBox 3](https://wiki.lyrion.org/index.php/Squeezebox_3rd_Generation.html)
- a [Logitech SqueezeBox Boom](https://lyrion.org/players-and-controllers/squeezebox-boom/)

so that I could listen to my MP3s on my family room stereo as well as in other rooms in the house.

Today, I listen to music using Amazon Alexa speaker connected Apple Music's streaming service because it is convenient. However, when I encounter music that I like enough, I buy the CD because I don't trust the longevity of any cloud-based service.

## Migrating Away from the VCR

In 2000, a few of us bought series 1 TiVos. Of course we added the Ethernet card that [Andrew Tridgell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andrew_Tridgell)
(of [Samba](https://www.samba.org) fame) [developed](https://www.samba.org/~tridge/tivo-ethernet/). And of course we added multiple larger hard drives.

In 2005, I migrated from TiVo to [MythTV](https://www.mythtv.org) because MythTV fit with my belief that audio and video should be distributed over an IP network. MythTV has backends that are responsible for recording the cable and over-the-air television and frontends that are responsible for playing the content. In addition to being able to record and play back television, MythTV could flag and skip television commercials. In addition television recording and playback, it could display images, play music, play videos, play DVDs and play video games.

In 2005, I started helping Larry Matter with [MiniMyth](https://www.mythtv.org/wiki/Minimyth), eventually taking the lead on MiniMyth's development. I gravitated toward MiniMyth because it was a network bootable image that turned a relatively inexpensive diskless VIA EPIA M mini-ITX motherboard into a dedicated MythTV frontend.

When I moved in 2016, I stopped using MythTV because I didn't want to deal with maintaining the server required. I replaced MythTV's non-TV functions with
[Plex] (<https://www.plex.tv>). running on my NAS. In 2020, I started using a [Nuvyyo Tablo QUAD 1TB DVR](https://www.engadget.com/tablo-dual-128gb-quad-1tb-announcement-211652423.html).
